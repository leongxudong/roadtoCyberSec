# roadtoCyberSec

Documenting my journey from cybersecurity foundations to information security governance, risk, compliance, assurance, and security management.

This repository serves as a structured record of my cybersecurity learning journey, including formal education, professional certifications, key topics studied, practical reflections, and future learning plans.

## About This Repository

Cybersecurity is broad. My pathway has moved across several layers:

* Technical foundations: networking, operating systems, security architecture, vulnerability assessment, and ethical hacking
* Governance, risk, and compliance: ISO/IEC 27001, PDPA, NIST CSF, COBIT, audit, vendor risk, and cyber maturity
* Security management: policy development, awareness, incident response, risk reporting, and stakeholder communication
* Cloud and Microsoft security: Microsoft 365, Entra ID, Intune, security compliance, and Azure security fundamentals
* Project and business alignment: translating security requirements into usable controls, operational processes, and defensible decisions

The aim is not only to collect certifications, but to show how each stage contributes to practical cybersecurity work.

* CIA triad: confidentiality, integrity, availability
* Authentication, authorization, and accounting

* Security operations and monitoring

* ISO/IEC 27001 and ISMS governance
* Risk assessment and treatment
* Security policies, standards, and procedures
* Vendor and third-party risk management

Topics covered:

* Audit planning and scoping
* Evidence gathering

* Stakeholder reporting

## Practical Reflections

Some recurring lessons across my cybersecurity journey:

1. Security controls must be usable, or users will work around them.
2. Risk management must be specific; vague risks do not lead to clear decisions.
3. Policies are only useful when they are translated into operating procedures.
4. Compliance is the floor, not the end-state.
5. Technical knowledge improves the quality of governance decisions.
6. Good security communication should be clear to non-technical stakeholders.
7. Vendor risk must be proportionate to the data, access, service dependency, and business impact.
8. Awareness training should be scenario-based and relevant to the user’s actual work.
9. Incident response depends on preparation, escalation paths, and role clarity.
10. Cyber maturity improves through repeatable processes, not one-off activities.
